Femicide and Gender-Based Violence Dashboard

This dashboard provides a comparative view of femicide trends, gender-based violence, SDG 5 (gender equality) performance, and media attention in Ireland, South Africa, and the United States. The goal is to show how structural gender inequality, lethal violence, and public discourse intersect over time.

Use the navigation links above to move between charts and sections. All visualisations are self-contained HTML exports and can be hosted on GitHub Pages without a backend.

Detailed Graph Descriptions

1. Long-Term Trends in Female Homicide Counts

File: long_term_female_homicide_counts.html

What the figure shows:
This visualisation presents the absolute number of female homicide victims per year in Ireland, South Africa, and the United States. It displays country-specific trajectories from 2015 onwards, enabling a direct comparison of the scale of femicides across national contexts. Each country is represented by a separate line, with annual data points marked.

Major findings:

  • South Africa reports the highest absolute femicide counts throughout the observed period, with annual totals often exceeding 2,500 victims.
  • The United States displays persistently high counts as well, ranging between roughly 3,300 and 5,000 victims per year.
  • Ireland’s absolute numbers are very low in comparison, typically varying between 2 and 15 victims annually.
  • All three countries show fluctuations, with notable increases in recent years for South Africa and the United States.

Data used:
ireland_female_homicide_counts.csv, south_africa_female_homicide_counts.csv, united_states_female_homicide_counts.csv

2. Long-Term Trends in Female Homicide Rates

File: long_term_female_homicide_rates.html

What the figure shows:
This chart depicts the annual femicide rate per 100,000 women for Ireland, South Africa, and the United States. By presenting population-adjusted rates, it enables a more accurate comparison of national femicide burdens across countries with vastly different population sizes.

Major findings:

  • South Africa consistently has the highest rates, with several years showing values above 9 per 100,000 women.
  • The United States shows medium-level rates, generally between 2 and 3 per 100,000 women.
  • Ireland’s rate is extremely low and remains below 1 per 100,000 women for the entire period.
  • Rates in South Africa rise sharply in 2021 and 2022, indicating a worsening situation.
  • Rates in Ireland and the United States remain more stable but show modest upward movements in the most recent years.

Data used:
ireland_female_homicide_rates.csv, south_africa_female_homicide_rates.csv, united_states_female_homicide_rates.csv

3. Media Mentions of “Femicide” and “Gender-Based Violence” by Country and Year

File: media_mentions_femicide_gbv_bar_chart.html

What the figure shows:
This visualisation aggregates yearly counts of media mentions for two terms—“femicide” and “gender-based violence”— across Ireland, South Africa, and the United States. It presents the results as grouped bar charts, with separate panels (facets) for each country.

Major findings:

  • Media attention varies enormously between countries.
  • The United States generates the highest number of mentions, especially regarding “gender-based violence,” with annual totals exceeding 700 cases in the most recent years.
  • South Africa shows substantial media coverage as well, particularly for “gender-based violence,” but lower than the United States.
  • Ireland exhibits comparatively low media attention for both terms, though a noticeable uptick in mentions occurs after 2020.
  • Mentions of “gender-based violence” consistently exceed those for “femicide” in all three countries.

Data used:
Irland_femicide.csv.csv, Irland_gender_based_violence.csv.csv, Sydafrika_femicide.csv.csv, Sydafrika_gender_based_violence.csv.csv, USA_femicide.csv.csv, USA_gender_based_violence.csv.csv

4. Multi-Country Femicide and SDG 5 Overlay (Including United States)

File: multi_country_femicide_sdg5_overlay_US_included.html

What the figure shows:
This dual-axis line chart overlays two types of indicators for each country—SDG 5 performance (institutional gender-equality score) and femicide rates per 100,000 women—displayed across time. SDG 5 scores are shown on the left axis, while femicide rates are plotted on the right axis.

Major findings:

  • Ireland and the United States show steady progress on SDG 5 scores over time, but femicide rates do not consistently decline in tandem.
  • South Africa displays higher volatility, with SDG 5 scores fluctuating more strongly and femicide rates remaining consistently elevated.
  • No country shows a clear one-to-one correspondence between improvements in institutional gender-equality measures and decreases in femicide rates.
  • The divergence between structural gender-equality indicators and lethal violence outcomes is particularly pronounced in South Africa.

Data used:
sdg_data.csv, ireland_female_homicide_rates.csv, south_africa_female_homicide_rates.csv, united_states_female_homicide_rates.csv

5. SDG Goal 5 Progress Over Time

File: sdg_goal5_progress_over_time.html

What the figure shows:
This line chart tracks SDG 5 (gender equality) scores for Ireland, South Africa, and the United States from 2000 to 2022. It provides a longitudinal view of institutional gender-equality commitments and progress.

Major findings:

  • Ireland and the United States show gradual, sustained increases in SDG 5 scores over the entire period, reflecting ongoing policy and institutional reforms.
  • South Africa begins at a relatively high score but shows moderate fluctuations across years, including some decline around 2018–2020.
  • All three countries converge at broadly similar SDG 5 levels by 2022 (mid-70s range).

Data used:
sdg_data.csv

6. Relationship Between SDG 5 Performance and Female Homicide Rates (Regression Analysis)

File: sdg5_vs_female_homicide_rate_regression.html

What the figure shows:
This scatter plot explores the association between SDG 5 scores and femicide rates across the three countries. Each point represents a country–year combination. A linear regression line (overall OLS trendline) is included to summarise the global relationship.

Major findings:

  • Ireland clusters in the upper-range SDG 5 scores with extremely low femicide rates.
  • South Africa clusters in the mid-to-high SDG 5 range but with very high femicide rates, showing that strong institutional frameworks do not automatically translate into lower lethal violence against women.
  • The United States sits between Ireland and South Africa on both dimensions.
  • The regression line has a positive slope, indicating that higher SDG 5 scores are not associated with lower femicide rates in this dataset.
  • The R² value is low (around 0.28), indicating weak explanatory power: institutional gender-equality scores do not reliably predict femicide rates.

Data used:
sdg_data.csv, ireland_female_homicide_rates.csv, south_africa_female_homicide_rates.csv, united_states_female_homicide_rates.csv

About the Data and Methods

This dashboard combines several sources:

  • Female homicide counts and rates from national statistical offices and UNODC.
  • SDG 5 scores from the SDG Index.
  • Media references to femicide and gender-based violence from targeted media searches.

The visualisations are intended to provide a structured empirical overview of femicide, gender-based violence, and institutional gender equality across three countries, to support qualitative and policy discussion rather than produce definitive causal estimates.
