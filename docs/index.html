Femicide and Gender-Based Violence Dashboard

Femicide refers to the killing of women and girls, often on account of their gender. In many countries, it is not recognised as a crime independent of homicide. The Sustainable Development Goals, or SDGs, are a set of 17 aims agreed upon by the United Nations, implemented in 2015 and hopefully to be achieved by 2030. SDG number 5 concerns gender equality, and has a specific list of indicators. The purpose of this research is to determine whether the SDG indicators are a sufficient measure of gender equality, by comparing rates and recognition of femicide to countries’ performances on SDG 5. There are three countries of varying performance being examined: Ireland, South Africa, and the United States of America.

Use the interactive graphs below to uncover the relationship between rates and recognition of femicide and performance on SDG 5.

Legal Recognition and Statistical Visibility

Across the three focus countries, femicide is only partially recognised in law and statistics. In Ireland, there is no legal definition of femicide and no dedicated national statistics, which limits systematic monitoring of such killings (McGoldrick, Collis, and Mulligan 2024). In the United States, the absence of a distinct penal category for gender-related killings likewise hampers efforts to track femicide (Lewis et al. 2024). By contrast, several Southern African states have adopted laws and strategies on gender-based violence and femicide, including South Africa’s National Strategic Plan on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide, Botswana’s Gender-Based Violence Act of 2008, and Lesotho’s Counter Domestic Violence Act of 2022, yet these measures are widely regarded as failing to curb lethal violence against women in practice (Ngube n.d.).

SDG 5 Indicators

SDG 5 is monitored through a set of core indicators covering legal equality, violence, harmful practices, unpaid work, political representation, reproductive health, access to resources, technology, and data systems.

  • 5.1.1 Legal frameworks promoting equality and non-discrimination.
  • 5.2.1 Violence by intimate partners against women (15+).
  • 5.2.2 Sexual violence by non-partners.
  • 5.3.1 Women married before age 15 or 18.
  • 5.3.2 Girls and women who have undergone FGM/C.
  • 5.4.1 Time spent on unpaid domestic and care work.
  • 5.5.1 Women’s representation in national parliaments and local governments.
  • 5.5.2 Women in managerial positions.
  • 5.6.1 Women making their own informed reproductive health decisions.
  • 5.6.2 Laws guaranteeing access to reproductive health care and information.
  • 5.a.1 Women’s ownership or secure rights over agricultural land.
  • 5.a.2 Equal legal rights to land ownership/control.
  • 5.b.1 Women’s mobile-phone ownership.
  • 5.c.1 Systems to track gender-equality commitments and data collection.

Gender-Based Violence and Femicide in South Africa

Gender-Based Violence and Femicide (GBVF) in South Africa remain among the most severe globally. This context is essential when interpreting the country’s comparatively strong SDG 5 score, which reflects formal commitments rather than lived realities.

Key Figures on GBVF in South Africa

  • The femicide rate is six times higher than the global average, with at least 15 women murdered every day.
  • In 2023/24, 5,578 women were murdered — a 33.8% increase from the previous year.
  • In the same period, 1,656 children were murdered — a 38.7% increase.
  • 42,569 rape cases were reported to the police in 2023/24, while an estimated 95% of rape cases go unreported.
  • 63,054 assaults with intent to inflict grievous bodily harm against women were recorded in 2023/24.

These figures illustrate the depth of the crisis and why statistical output alone cannot fully capture the gender-equality situation in South Africa.

Civil-Society Response: Women for Change

Women for Change (WFC), founded in 2016, is one of South Africa’s most influential non-profit organisations focused on combating GBVF. WFC leads national campaigns, public demonstrations, and community mobilisation efforts to raise awareness and advocate for stronger institutional responses.

How Gender-Equality Indices Interpret South Africa

SDG 5 (Gender Equality): A UN goal centred on legal frameworks, political representation, and broad development targets. South Africa is currently assessed as having achieved SDG 5, while the US and Ireland still face significant challenges.

Why SDG 5 can mislead: SDG 5 does not measure gender inequality adequately. Its limited and uneven indicators reward legal commitments rather than lived conditions. This helps explain why South Africa appears “on track” despite extremely high levels of GBVF and persistent socio-economic inequalities.

GII (Gender Inequality Index): A composite metric measuring inequality in reproductive health, empowerment, and labour markets.

EU Gender Equality Index: Tracks six domains—work, money, knowledge, time, power, and health—and includes two cross-cutting areas: violence and intersecting inequalities.

Together, these frameworks show that while South Africa performs strongly on some institutional indicators, alternative indices reveal significant disparities that align more closely with the lived experience reflected in GBVF data.

About the Data and Methods

This dashboard combines several sources:

  • Female homicide counts and rates from national statistical offices and UNODC.
  • SDG 5 scores from the SDG Index.
  • Media references to femicide and gender-based violence from targeted media searches.

The visualisations are intended to provide a structured empirical overview of femicide, gender-based violence, and institutional gender equality across three countries, to support qualitative and policy discussion rather than produce definitive causal estimates.

Overall Findings

Among the countries examined, South Africa is the only one that legally recognises femicide. It also performs highest on SDG 5, yet simultaneously has the highest femicide rate. Femicide rates have increased in all three countries since 2015, with South Africa showing the most substantial rise and the United States and Ireland experiencing more modest growth. Absolute counts have risen sharply in the United States, although this is heavily influenced by its population size.

In terms of recognition and public discussion, South Africa legally defines femicide but does not exhibit strong visibility in public discourse. Google Trends data shows rising interest in all countries, yet South Africa’s search activity for “femicide” and “gender-based violence” is similar to Ireland’s despite its much larger population. Newspaper headline data for South Africa could not be obtained. The United States shows far greater media engagement on both terms than Ireland, with increasing mentions since 2015, while Ireland has seen little change.

SDG 5 scores have increased in all countries since 2015, with South Africa currently ranked highest according to the SDG Index. However, the core question of whether SDG 5 performance correlates with other gender-equality indicators—such as femicide—receives little support from the data. The country facing the most severe femicide problem is also the country where the issue appears least publicly discussed, and where SDG 5 performance is officially strongest. The United States and Ireland also show rising femicide rates alongside rising SDG 5 scores.

Overall, SDG 5 appears built on an insufficient set of indicators. It does not adequately capture several key dimensions of gender inequality, including some of the most critical forms of harm.

About the Data and Methods

This dashboard draws on multiple data sources. Female homicide counts and rates come from national statistical agencies and the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC). SDG 5 scores are taken from the SDG Index. Media references to femicide and gender-based violence are obtained from targeted media searches, and search-interest patterns are derived from Google Trends.

The visualisations provide a structured empirical overview of femicide, gender-based violence, and institutional gender equality across Ireland, South Africa, and the United States. Their purpose is to support qualitative interpretation and policy debate, not to make causal claims. In this sense, the dashboard functions similarly to academic and public-policy data platforms: it informs discussion, facilitates comparison across time and context, and highlights emerging patterns without asserting definitive causal explanations.
